You’re right. I did not answer the first question directly enough.

Here is the direct answer.

First question: how would they sue GHS if GHS has nothing to do with their images?

They would usually not say:

“GHS stored my image, so GHS is automatically guilty.”

They would more likely say one or more of these:

GHS enabled or facilitated misuse

GHS processed and transformed my image/voice without proper safeguards

GHS helped create a misleading or harmful output

GHS failed to block obvious unauthorized use

GHS exported or packaged infringing content

GHS designed the tool in a way that encouraged misuse


That is the practical risk.

So even if:

GHS does not post it,

GHS does not keep it permanently,

GHS processes more on-device,


someone can still try to bring a claim if they believe your tool helped create an unlawful result involving their likeness, voice, privacy, or copyright. WIPO notes that image/personality/publicity-type rights can protect things like a person’s image and voice under different legal systems.

In plain words

Think of it like this:

If someone uses GHS to make:

a fake endorsement ad,

a cloned voice message,

a misleading video edit,

or a commercial using somebody else’s face,


that person may sue:

the user first,

but may also include GHS if they think GHS helped enable it, failed to stop it, or benefited from it.


That does not mean they automatically win against GHS.
It means GHS can still be dragged into the problem.

What helps GHS strongly

Your idea helps a lot:

keep work on device first

do not upload media by default

upload only after user approval

store only what is necessary

let users delete their assets

keep server retention minimal


That reduces privacy and storage risk.

But it does not fully protect GHS by itself, because the complaint can still be about:

unauthorized likeness use

unauthorized voice use

misleading/deceptive edits

copyright/licensing misuse


Best prevention stack

To reduce the chance of GHS being targeted, do these together:

1. On-device-first design

preview locally where possible

avoid automatic cloud upload

upload only after approval

show clear “stored locally / uploaded” status


2. Rights confirmation

Before risky actions, require user confirmation:

I own this content or have permission

I have rights to use this face/voice

I have commercial rights if this is an ad

I accept responsibility for unauthorized use


3. Strong blocking

Block by default:

celebrity cloning

third-party voice cloning without explicit permission

fake endorsement ads

deceptive “make this person say this” edits

intimate/degrading manipulations

child-risk content


4. Review-first workflow

No final export/publish until:

preview shown

user approves

rights check passed

safety check passed

license check passed


5. Audit logs

Keep records of:

user approval

rights-confirmation version

whether media stayed local or was uploaded

sound license used

attribution generated

export time

provider used


6. Clear terms

State clearly:

GHS is a tool

users are responsible for rights they do not own

GHS may block suspicious requests

GHS may suspend misuse


The strongest practical answer

So the honest answer is:

They could still sue GHS by claiming GHS helped enable unauthorized or harmful use of their likeness, voice, or content, even if GHS did not post it and did not keep it permanently.

And the best defense is:

on-device-first + approval-before-upload + strong rights checks + strict blocking + audit logs + review-first export.

Very important correction

Do not rely on this argument alone:

> “GHS has nothing to do with their images.”



That is too weak legally and product-wise.

A better position is:

Users remain responsible for content they do not own or lack permission to use.”



That is much stronger.
