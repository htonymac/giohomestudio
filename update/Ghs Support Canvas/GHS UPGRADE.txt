2. Can people sue GHS Content Creator?

Yes. They can try.

The more useful question is: what creates the legal risk?

The biggest risk is not only server upload. Risk can still exist even if files stay mostly on the phone, because lawsuits can come from:

unauthorized use of someone’s image/likeness

unauthorized use of someone’s voice

misleading endorsements

privacy violations

copyright infringement

breach of platform rules

defamation or deceptive edits


Image rights and similar protections vary by country, but WIPO notes that these rights are recognized under different legal frameworks, including image rights, personality rights, and publicity rights. WIPO materials also list voice among protectable personality/publicity-related attributes. 

So this part is important:

Keeping content on-device reduces some storage and breach risk, but it does not remove rights risk.

What on-device processing helps with

It can reduce:

unnecessary server exposure

data retention risk

breach surface area

“you stored my private media” complaints


What it does not solve by itself

It does not automatically solve:

copyright issues

image/likeness rights

voice cloning rights

misuse of third-party content

harmful/deceptive output


So your idea is still good:

process as much as possible on device

only upload after user approval

store user-approved assets under the user’s own panel

minimize what GHS retains


But that should be treated as a privacy/security decision, not a full legal shield.

3. If GHS stores only after approval, does that help?

Yes, it helps.

That is a cleaner privacy model than “upload everything first.”

If you do use R2, Cloudflare says objects in R2 are encrypted at rest automatically, and R2 also supports SSE-C if you want customer-provided server-side encryption keys. 

But again, encryption helps with security, not with whether the user had the right to use the content in the first place.

4. Free sounds from Freesound and similar sites

This is where you need discipline.

Freesound says sounds on the site may use different licenses, including:

CC0

CC BY

CC BY-NC 


Their own summary says:

CC0: you can do pretty much what you want

CC BY: you must credit the original creator

CC BY-NC: attribution is required and you cannot earn money from the work using it unless you have separate permission 


So the direct answer is:

Can customers be sued for using sounds from Freesound or other free sound sites?

Yes, if they use the wrong license wrongly.

Examples:

using CC BY without attribution

using CC BY-NC in a commercial ad

using a sound that was wrongly uploaded by someone who did not own it

using a platform’s content outside its terms


Freesound itself warns that user-uploaded content can sometimes be illegal or wrongly uploaded. 

So your safest product rule is:

Recommended sound-source policy

Use 3 buckets only:

1. Fully owned / custom-created sounds


2. CC0 sounds


3. CC BY sounds with automatic attribution support



Avoid using CC BY-NC in normal business/commercial production workflows unless there is explicit separate permission.

That is the practical rule.

5. “Customer should know custom sound is theirs and no one can penalize them”

Be careful with that wording.

You should not promise:

“no one can penalize them”

“they fully own everything”

“nobody can sue”


That is too strong.

Instead say something like:

Custom-generated sound created inside GHS may be used by the customer subject to GHS terms and any third-party provider terms.

For imported third-party sounds, the customer is responsible for following the applicable license and attribution requirements.


Why? Because if a sound includes third-party material, or a provider has usage restrictions, you do not want to overpromise.

7. Can Claude/GPT work with FFmpeg during assembly?

Yes. Absolutely.

The clean way to think about it is:

LLM/AI plans

FFmpeg executes


The AI can decide:

where narration starts

where music drops in

where SFX should land

how loud each track should be

where subtitle timing should go

where overlays should appear

where cuts happen


Then FFmpeg does the actual assembly:

audio mixing

trimming

concat

overlay

subtitles

timing alignment

final export


That is very possible. FFmpeg’s docs show support for:

mixing multiple audio inputs with amix

overlaying images/logos/video

subtitle rendering

concatenation workflows 


So yes: AI + FFmpeg is a strong architecture.

But the LLM should not directly “freestyle” the whole assembly. Better architecture is:

AI creates an assembly plan → structured timeline JSON → FFmpeg command builder executes it

That will be far more stable than asking the AI to improvise raw FFmpeg every time.
