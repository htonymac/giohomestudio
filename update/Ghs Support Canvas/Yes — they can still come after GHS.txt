Yes — they can still come after GHS, even if GHS is not the one posting publicly or permanently storing the media.

Why? Because a product can still be accused of facilitating unlawful use if it provides the tool, processes the material, generates altered outputs, clones a voice, adds endorsements, or assembles misleading content. The risk is higher when the app helps create identifiable uses of a person’s image, name, or voice, because WIPO notes that these kinds of attributes are protected under different legal frameworks such as image rights, personality rights, and publicity rights, and WIPO materials also list voice among the protected personal attributes in this area. 

So your point is partly right:

not uploading everything by default helps

not posting for the user helps

keeping more work on-device helps


But that only reduces some risk. It does not fully remove risk if GHS:

helps create unauthorized likeness/voice outputs,

helps create misleading endorsements,

helps assemble defamatory or deceptive edits,

or uses third-party sounds/images under the wrong license terms. 


How they could still sue GHS

The typical argument would not be “you stored my photo forever.”
It would more likely be:

your software enabled unauthorized use of my face or voice,

your software generated a misleading version of me,

your software helped package and export infringing material,

your software failed to warn/block obviously risky use,

or your product design encouraged misuse without proper controls.


That does not mean they would win. It means GHS can still be dragged into the dispute, spend money on defense, or face takedown/platform pressure.

Best way to reduce that risk

You need product controls, not just legal text.

The strongest prevention stack is:

1. On-device-first where possible

Do:

import and preview on device first

analyze locally where feasible

upload only after user approval

let the user delete approved assets

keep server retention minimal


This helps privacy and reduces exposure if something sensitive is involved.

2. Rights confirmation before risky use

Before voice cloning, face-based generation, endorsement-style ads, or third-party media transformation, require the user to confirm:

I own this content or have permission

I have permission to use this likeness/voice

I have commercial rights if this is an ad

I understand I am responsible for false or unauthorized use


3. Strict blocking rules

Block by default:

celebrity cloning

third-party voice cloning without explicit rights confirmation

“make this person say…” fraud-style requests

fake testimonial/ad endorsement edits

non-consensual intimate or misleading edits

child-risk content


4. Audit logs

Store logs for:

source type

whether it stayed local or was uploaded

user approval moment

rights-confirmation checkbox version

sound license used

attribution text generated

provider used

export time


5. Review-first workflow

No final render, upload, or publish until:

user previewed,

user approved,

license checks passed,

rights checks passed,

safety checks passed.


That is one of your best legal and product defenses.
